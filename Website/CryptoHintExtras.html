Discussions

The idea for CryptoHint was inspired by online tools designed to solve cryptograms. At first I was going to create an iPhone app to solve cryptograms, but realized attempting to type in an entire cryptogram consisting of apparently randomized letters would be a chore; besides that, the fun of solving a cryptogram is eliminated by a tool that does it for you. It is better to have a tool to help solve a cryptogram but not do everything for you, and typing in one word at a time wouldn't be so bad. There are cryptogram dictionaries that exist in book form, though an Internet search only found one, published in 2007, and its wordlist is somewhat limited. Despite comments I've found online that a programmer could easily write a program to generate a list of cryptogram letter patterns, I didn't find anything remotely like it in the iPhone App Store. And in the user reviews for one of the cryptogram puzzle apps, a reviewer mentioned a nice feature to have would be to look up words that would match a cryptogram's letter pattern. It seemed obvious there could be a market for an app that could do such lookups.

The addition of the list of recent cryptogram words came after testing an earlier version of CryptoHint against real cryptograms and crypto family puzzles. At the start of a given puzzle a word might have an excessively long list of matches and I'd try other words in the puzzle; I'd eventually need to reenter the previous word after a while, which could be a pain. I thought, 'Why can't the app remember what I tried earlier so I don't need to retype it?' So I added the feature and saved a lot of retyping.

The use of picker wheels to specify letter substitution pairs eliminates the drudgery of having two text fields for the letter pairs, parsing to account for multiple letter entries, punctuation, numeric digits, checks for letters already used, and so on. Again, saves a lot of typing. Afterwards I found a cryptogram puzzle app that does the same thing with picker wheels, so it's not an original idea.

CryptoHint uses Courier and Marker Felt fonts to differentiate between cryptogram ciphertext and solution plaintext wherever possible. When I started learning how to solve cryptograms by hand so many years ago I'd solve the cryptograms in the newspaper by writing the solution directly underneath the printed puzzle. Courier is the closest to what appears in newspaper cryptogram puzzles, and Marker Felt is the only available font on the iPhone that has a handlettered look. For the display of the substitution map, I had to make do with Courier in italics since Marker Felt is not a monospaced font.

Future Additions

Would like to make CryptoHint a Universal app that would run on and take advantage of the extra screen space of an iPad.

One 'nice to have' feature would be the ability to type in a cryptogram and have its letters replaced with the solution as the substitution map is filled in. The major difficulty is the difficulty of typing all those encrypted letters on an iPhone virtual keyboard without errors. A minor difficulty would be finding available screen space.
